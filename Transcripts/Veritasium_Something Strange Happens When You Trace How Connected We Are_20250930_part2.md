## Key Concepts

**Prisoner's Dilemma**  
A game theory paradigm where two players must decide whether to cooperate or defect

**Cooperation**  
A behavior where individuals work together for mutual benefit

**Network Science**  
The study of complex networks and their properties

## Cross-Domain Connections

**Economics → Biology**

*Cooperation in biological systems and economic systems share similar structural patterns*

The Prisoner's Dilemma in economics describes how individual self-interest can lead to suboptimal outcomes, while in biology, the evolution of cooperation can be understood through similar game-theoretic lenses, highlighting the tension between individual and group benefits. This analogy reveals that cooperation can emerge in both domains despite individual incentives to defect. The structural pattern of cooperation emerging from individual interactions is a key insight here.

**Economics → Computer Science**

*Principles of network science apply to understanding network structures in computer science*

The study of network science in economics focuses on how nodes and edges interact to produce system-level behaviors. Similarly, in computer science, understanding network topology is crucial for designing and optimizing communication networks. The principle of network science—focusing on the interactions and structures of networks—transfers directly to computer science, where it informs the design of more efficient and resilient networks.

**Economics → Social Psychology**

*The concept of cooperation in economics can be metaphorically applied to understanding group dynamics in social psychology*

Cooperation in economics often involves individuals or groups working together to achieve a common goal, despite potential individual incentives to defect. This concept can be metaphorically extended to social psychology, where understanding how groups form, maintain, and dissolve can be informed by economic models of cooperation. The metaphor highlights the importance of reciprocity, trust, and communication in both economic and social contexts.
