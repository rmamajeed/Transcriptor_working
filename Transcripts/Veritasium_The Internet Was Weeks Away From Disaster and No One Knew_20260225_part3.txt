00:00 - 00:09 **Narrator:** ...the open-source community pored over countless small, similar projects looking for similar campaigns, but they found almost nothing.

There are state-sponsored parts of either governments, militaries, or even private contractors working for states, that are all preparing for the next cyber-escalation, some kind of war, some kind of a geopolitical conflict. And where are all of these backdoors? There are just too many people incentivized to put backdoors for the few backdoors that we are actually discovering.

00:35 - 00:58 **Speaker 2:** Now, some experts have argued this reveals a fundamental flaw in the open-source model. But not everyone agrees. Closed-source software would be no better here. In fact, who's to say that there aren't already state spies working as paid software engineers at some of the larger companies, putting in exactly backdoors like this? But then there would be no community member running free testing and detecting this by chance.

00:59 - 01:21 **Narrator:** This backdoor, if anything, underlines the ethos of open source. I mean, just think of what it took to get this done in public. There was a multi-year social engineering campaign, there were all these layers of misdirection, and then there was code that was designed to withstand constant scrutiny. Compare that now with a closed-source hack. Sometimes all it takes to get a backdoor installed there is a court order.

01:22 - 01:54 **Speaker 3:** Or you have a public company that can just brush a breach under the rug. I actually used to work as an open-source researcher myself at the Japanese telecom giant NTT. And my perspective is that it's only because this is an open-source project that it's been picked apart, analyzed, and turned into a conversation about security at all, one that focuses on the fundamental vulnerability. It's not the code; it's the people. And now, the system has not supported them enough.

01:55 - 02:32 **Speaker 4:** I feel for Lasse, that he's given this beautiful gift to the whole world, and what have we, what has humanity done back to him? Right? We've poisoned his gift and then I think implicitly a little bit, not everyone's saying this but implicitly, we're blaming him for not being there to maintain this stuff for free, forever. But why are we demanding that Lasse do anything when he's not being paid for this stuff? And that's, in my opinion, quite unfair.
