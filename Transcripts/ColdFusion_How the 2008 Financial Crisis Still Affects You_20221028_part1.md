## Key Concepts

**Financial Crisis**  
A situation where the financial system is unable to function properly, leading to economic instability

**Subprime Loans**  
Loans given to borrowers with poor credit history, often with high interest rates and risky terms

**Mortgage-Backed Securities**  
Financial instruments that represent an ownership interest in a pool of mortgage loans

**Glass-Steagall Act**  
A law that separated commercial and investment banking, repealed in 1999

**Collateralized Debt Obligation (CDO)**  
A financial instrument that represents a claim on a pool of debt obligations, often used to package subprime loans

## Cross-Domain Connections

**Economics → Biology**

*Contagion models can explain both financial crises and disease outbreaks*

Both subprime loans and pandemics exhibit rapid, unchecked growth due to interconnectedness and delayed feedback. This structural similarity allows for the application of contagion models from biology to understand the spread of financial crises. The concept of herd immunity in biology can be analogous to the idea of systemic risk in economics, where the failure of one entity can lead to a cascade of failures throughout the system.

**Economics → Computer Science**

*Both CDOs and data compression involve bundling and prioritizing components to manage risk and efficiency*

CDOs bundle assets to manage risk, while data compression algorithms bundle data to prioritize and reduce storage needs. This principle of bundling and prioritization can be applied across domains to manage complexity and optimize performance. In computer science, this principle can be used to develop more efficient data compression algorithms, while in economics, it can inform the development of more effective financial instruments.

**Economics → Engineering**

*Mortgage-backed securities can be seen as a structural system, where individual components contribute to overall stability*

Just as a building's structural integrity relies on the strength of its individual components, mortgage-backed securities rely on the creditworthiness of the underlying mortgages. This metaphor highlights the importance of understanding the interconnectedness of components in both economics and engineering. By applying principles from engineering, such as stress testing and fault tolerance, economists can better design and regulate financial systems to withstand shocks and stresses.
