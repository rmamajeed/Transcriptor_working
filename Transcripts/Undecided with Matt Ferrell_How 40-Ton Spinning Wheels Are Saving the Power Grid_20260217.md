Mechanical batteries that store energy as inertia

Network of power transmission and distribution lines

## Cross-Domain Connections

**Energy → Mechanical Engineering**

*Flywheel Energy Storage Systems and gyroscopes both rely on rotational kinetic energy for their operation.*

The principle of conservation of angular momentum is crucial in both flywheel energy storage systems and gyroscopes. This principle allows for the efficient storage and release of energy in flywheels, and enables gyroscopes to maintain their orientation in space. The structural analogy between these two concepts highlights the importance of understanding rotational dynamics in different fields.

**Energy → Economics**

*The integration of renewable energy sources into the power grid can be seen as a supply-side response to the increasing demand for energy.*

The principle of supply and demand is fundamental in economics, and the growth of renewable energy can be understood as a shift in the supply curve. As renewable energy becomes more cost-competitive, it increases the overall supply of energy, which can lead to a decrease in prices and an increase in consumption. This principle can be applied to other fields, such as the development of new technologies or the expansion of existing industries.

**Energy → Computer Science**

*The power grid can be seen as a complex network, similar to computer networks, with nodes and edges that need to be optimized for efficient energy transfer.*

The metaphor of a network can be applied to the power grid, where power plants and transmission lines are nodes and edges, respectively. This perspective can help in understanding the complexity of the power grid and in developing strategies for optimizing energy distribution. Similarly, concepts from network topology, such as centrality and clustering, can be applied to the study of the power grid.

10:33 - Matt Ferrell: As neat as flywheels are, they're far from perfect. Their most obvious flaw is that they're constantly fighting against friction. Even the most souped-up, carbon-fibered-bodied, magnetic-bearing-to-vacuum-chambered flywheel will encounter some kind of friction that will eventually slow it down. Flywheels lose about 5% to 20% of their energy per hour, so long-term storage is not really an option. That's why they're often paired with batteries, but of course that means additional engineering challenges and costs. And speaking of costs, we've got a bit of a mixed bag. Flywheels have long lives and don't need a ton of maintenance due to their simpler working principle. That does save costs in the long run. However, they're very expensive upfront. By necessity, flywheels need to be made from materials that can stand up to the incredible speeds and the weights that are being thrown around. Often it's carbon fiber, which isn't too expensive but also isn't cheap. And for best results, we want to cheat the physics of friction and optimize our flywheel's performance. This is fairly simple to do: we just mentioned magnetic bearings, vacuum chambers, and the like. This is especially important when your entire country is relying on the flywheel energy storage system to prevent another deadly blackout. But custom-optimized systems are going to come with custom-optimized price tags. Now, worse than those upfront costs is the danger element. Flywheels are very safe, but they're very heavy objects spinning at very fast speeds. Should something go wrong, they can instantly become massive kinetic missiles that can destroy everything in their path. Heck, even if a flywheel doesn't break free of its housing, it can occasionally tear itself apart, flinging around many small kinetic shards. This means that flywheels have to be placed very carefully, and it's often best practice to place them in special wells. Excavating these wells isn't cheap, and they can really drive up the overall cost. But to be clear: the chances of a catastrophic oopsie are kind of slim, though it's not best to take a chance.
